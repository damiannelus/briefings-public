## Macro Spotlight – Deep Dive

The December 2025 cyberattack on Poland’s energy infrastructure marks a pivotal moment in national security and technological resilience. Attackers, identified as highly skilled, compromised at least 30 renewable energy and combined heat and power plants, impacting 500,000 consumers by exploiting default Fortigate device credentials and unpatched firmware. Although power generation was not interrupted, the breach exposed critical vulnerabilities in communication systems between operators and energy assets, threatening the stability of the entire electricity grid (source: ["Jak Rosjanie zhackowali polski sektor energetyczny miesiąc temu?"](https://niebezpiecznik.pl/post/rosja-atak-polska-energetyka-oze-osd/), ["Kto i w jaki sposób zaatakował w grudniu 2025 polską infrastrukturę energetyczną"](https://zaufanatrzeciastrona.pl/post/kto-i-w-jaki-sposob-zaatakowal-w-grudniu-2025-polska-infrastrukture-energetyczna/)).

Simultaneously, Poland is advancing its renewable energy agenda with European Investment Bank backing for three major offshore wind projects—Baltica 2, Bałtyk 2 and 3, and BC Wind—expected to supply electricity to 5.6 million households, or one-third of the country’s total (source: ["Zdaniem Europejskiego Banku Inwestycyjnego 1/3 polskich gospodarstw w Polsce może otrzymać zasilanie z wiatru"](https://www.gospodarkamorska.pl/zdaniem-europejskiego-banku-inwestycyjnego-13-polskich-gospodarstw-w-polsce-moze-otrzymac-zasilanie-z-wiatru-90319)). This scale of renewable integration demands a simultaneous upgrade in grid security and legislative reform, as outlined in 2026’s planned systemic and legal changes targeting connection systems and energy storage solutions (source: ["OZE – 2026 to rok ważnych zmian systemowych i prawnych"](https://www.gospodarkamorska.pl/oze-2026-to-rok-waznych-zmian-systemowych-i-prawnych-90318)).

Three scenarios emerge: if cybersecurity measures accelerate alongside renewable deployment, Poland could achieve a resilient, decarbonized energy system that supports economic growth and energy independence. Conversely, if security lags behind infrastructure expansion, the risk of destabilizing attacks grows, potentially causing blackouts and economic disruption. A stagnation or rollback in reforms would undermine both energy transition and national security, eroding investor confidence and slowing GDP growth, which in 2025 was a modest 3.6%—below expectations (source: ["PKB Polski na trójkę z solidnym plusem"](https://www.bankier.pl/wiadomosc/Wzrost-PKB-Polski-w-2025-roku-wyniosl-3-6-To-wzrost-ponizej-naszych-aspiracji-9076633.html)).

For business leaders, investors, and innovation managers, this means prioritizing cybersecurity investments in legacy and new energy assets, aligning with the 2026 legislative reforms to safeguard infrastructure. CTOs should integrate AI-driven monitoring and patch management to prevent exploits like those used in the Fortigate attack. Investors must evaluate energy projects not only on capacity but on resilience metrics. Entrepreneurs and innovation leaders should focus on developing secure, scalable energy software solutions that meet evolving regulatory demands. Monitoring Poland’s GDP growth trajectory and industrial modernization efforts, including the 15 billion złoty System San defense contract, will be critical for strategic planning over the next 12 to 36 months.
